What is a Peak Sun Hour?

A Peak Sun Hour is not a literal measurement of daylight hours (sunrise to sunset). It is a measurement of solar energy intensity.

1 Peak Sun Hour = 1 hour of sunlight at 1,000 W/m²

This is the Standard Test Condition (STC) used to rate solar panel output.

The sun's intensity follows a bell curve throughout the day — starting low at sunrise, peaking around solar noon, and waning as it sets. PSH converts this variable curve into an equivalent number of "perfect" hours.

Example: If your roof receives 500 W/m² for 2 hours in the early morning, those two hours combine to equal exactly one Peak Sun Hour.

Philippines: 4.0 hours is the conservative standard

The raw geographic average is 4.5–5.5 hours, but Philippine solar installers use 4.0 hours to safely account for cloud cover, rainy seasons (Habagat), panel degradation, and wiring losses.

The Formulas

Basic Formula

PSH = Total Daily Solar Insolation (Wh/m²) / 1,000 W/m²

Since 1,000 Wh = 1 kWh, if a database provides insolation in kWh/m²/day, PSH equals that number directly.

PSH = Total Daily Solar Insolation (kWh/m²)

Rigorous Definition (Integral Form)

To account for the sun's varying intensity throughout the day, engineers use:

PSH = ∫sunrisesunset G(t) / GSTC dt


Where:

  G(t)    = Actual solar irradiance at time t (W/m²)

  GSTC  = Standard reference irradiance (1,000 W/m²)

Extraterrestrial Radiation H0 (Latitude-Based Theoretical Maximum)

This formula calculates how much solar energy hits the top of the atmosphere at your latitude — before clouds and weather interfere:

H0 = (24 × 60 / π) × Gsc × dr × (ωs × sin(φ) × sin(δ) + cos(φ) × cos(δ) × sin(ωs))


Where:

  φ      = Your Latitude (radians)

  Gsc    = Solar constant (1,367 W/m²)

  dr     = Inverse relative Earth-Sun distance (varies by day of year)

  δ      = Solar declination (Earth's tilt, varies by day)

  ωs    = Sunset hour angle = arccos(-tan(φ) × tan(δ))

For the Philippines (~13–15° N): This gives 9–10 theoretical PSH in summer. But you don't actually get 9 hours of perfect sunlight on the ground — atmosphere, clouds, humidity, and pollution cut it roughly in half.

Hargreaves-Samani Model (Practical Estimation)

Combines latitude with temperature data to estimate actual ground-level sunlight:

Rs = kRs × √(Tmax - Tmin) × H0


Where:

  Rs      = Actual solar radiation (ground-level PSH)

  H0      = Extraterrestrial radiation (from latitude formula above)

  Tmax    = Daily maximum temperature

  Tmin    = Daily minimum temperature

  kRs    = 0.16 (inland) or 0.19 (coastal Philippines)

Why Satellite Data Beats Formulas

While these formulas are elegant physics, calculating them by hand is obsolete for designing solar systems. The Philippines has a distinct dry season (Amihan) and a heavily clouded wet season (Habagat). A latitude-based formula cannot accurately predict the sunlight blocked by July and August monsoon clouds.

This is why the industry relies on satellite-based databases that physically measure clouds and weather over a 20-year period to give a true, grounded average.

Panel Tilt Angle & Orientation

While you shouldn't use latitude alone to calculate sunlight hours, you must use latitude to calculate the tilt angle of your solar panels.

All panels in the Philippines should face SOUTH

The Philippines is north of the equator (4°–21° N), so panels must face toward the equator (South) to capture the most sunlight.

Optimal Tilt Formula

Year-round optimal: Tilt ≈ Latitude

Dry season (Nov–Apr): Tilt = Latitude - 15° (flatter)

Wet season (May–Oct): Tilt = Latitude + 15° (steeper, sheds rain)

Get Your Exact PSH Data

For hyper-local data rather than regional averages, use these free satellite-based databases:

1. Global Solar Atlas (World Bank Group)

globalsolaratlas.info →

How to use: Search for your exact Philippine location. Look for Global Horizontal Irradiation (GHI) or Direct Normal Irradiation (DNI). The daily GHI value in kWh/m² is your Peak Sun Hours.

2. NREL PVWatts Calculator

pvwatts.nrel.gov →

How to use: Enter your address or coordinates. View "Solar Radiation (kWh/m²/day)" broken down month-by-month. This reveals how much PSH drops during Habagat (wet season) versus the dry season.

3. Solargis Philippines Map

solargis.com/maps →

How to use: Download free high-resolution solar radiation maps of the Philippines showing kWh/m² distribution across the archipelago.
